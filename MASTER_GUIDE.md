## 1. Foundation: Why Growth Wins & The New Approach {#1-foundation}

**Core belief:** "Poor distribution, not product, is the number one cause of failure." — Peter Thiel

Today you need **great tech + great product + great distribution** to win.

### Why Growth Wins (4 Reasons)

- **Defensibility:** More distribution leads to more leverage into other products and more growth
- **Resources:** Growth attracts capital and talent; applied well, it produces more growth
- **Learning:** Learnings compound; growth produces quicker, deeper learning
- **Compounding:** Growth begets growth when you use the right frameworks

### The Game Has Changed

- Distribution is more competitive and expensive (CAC up 55–65%)
- Channel/tactic lifecycle has accelerated
- Access to data increased, costs decreased
- Product / marketing / engineering / sales lines have blurred — growth is everyone's job

### The System You Must Build

Your growth engine must be:

- **Systematic:** Methodical, not throwing things against the wall
- **Deterministic:** Not leaving growth to luck
- **Sustainable:** No shark-fin graphs; built to last
- **Repeatable:** Steps you can repeat to adapt to changes

**Output:** Growth = Retention + Acquisition + Monetization (as one cohesive system, not silos)

## 2. Phase 1: Define Retention (The Power Plant) {#2-phase-1-retention}

**Rule #1:** Retention separates the top 1% from the rest. It powers acquisition and monetization — not the other way around.

### Step 1.1 — Retention Is the Output

Retention answers: *"How many users/customers remained active within a defined time period after signing up?"*

Retention is binary: active or not. The **inputs** to improve retention are: **Activation, Engagement, Resurrection**.

### Step 1.2 — Build the Qualitative Use Case First

Work from the inside out. Define:

- **Problem:** What problem does your product solve?
- **Persona:** Who is your target user?
- **Alternative + Natural Frequency:** What do they do today? How often do they have this problem?
- **Core action:** What action proves you're delivering value?

### Step 1.3 — Define Your Retention Metric

Avoid these three mistakes:

- Setting wrong **frequency** (don't copy competitors blindly)
- Choosing wrong **core action**
- Optimizing for the wrong **audience**

### Step 1.4 — Visualize With Cohorts & Retention Curves

- Use cohort analysis to see how groups perform over time
- Plot retention curves to see where you flatten
- Identify impact of incorrect retention metrics before optimizing

**Tip:** If you're working on a feature, treat it as "a product within a product" and apply the same retention thinking.

## 3. Phase 2: Build Engagement & Habit Loops {#3-phase-2-engagement}

### Step 2.1 — Define Your Organic Habit Loop

The habit loop (Cue to Routine to Reward) drives engagement:

- **Trigger:** Stems from the problem in your use case — when does the user naturally need you?
- **Action:** Core action from your retention metric
- **Reward:** Why they choose you over the alternative — this reinforces the habit

*Examples: Pinterest — "I'm bored" then visit Pinterest and pin then get content; Zoom — "I need a meeting" then host on Zoom then high-quality, seamless experience*

### Step 2.2 — Reinforce the Habit

Use:

- **Manufactured loops:** Push notifications, emails, in-app prompts
- **Environment loops:** Physical/environmental cues (e.g., browser extension for Zoom)
- Combine both where it makes sense

### Step 2.3 — Activation (The Three Moments)

Get users to the "engaged" state by nailing:

- **Setup moment:** First-time setup friction
- **Aha moment:** When they realize value
- **Habit moment:** When they establish the organic loop

### Step 2.4 — Resurrection (When to Focus)

- **Involuntary dormant:** Life got in the way — re-engage with reminders
- **Voluntary dormant:** Chose to leave — need different approach (value, incentives)

## 4. Phase 3: Design Acquisition Loops {#4-phase-3-acquisition}

**Don't think funnels. Think loops.** Funnels push linear activities; loops compound. If you aren't compounding, you are dying.

### Step 3.1 — Three Guiding Principles

1. **Channel dynamics (law of habit transfer):** Every company is built off another channel. Insert yourself where your audience already has habits.
2. **Product-channel fit:** Products are built for channels; channels do not mold to products. Design product for the channel from the start.
3. **Channel-model fit:** Your monetization model enables or disables channels. Low ARPU means low CAC channels (virality, UGC). High ARPU means paid/sales.

### Step 3.2 — Map Your Acquisition Loops

Loop = Input to Steps to Output (reinvested in input). Four categories:

| Category | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Viral | User invites friends (LinkedIn, Slack, Dropbox) |
| Content (UGC) | Users create content, indexed, new users find it (Pinterest, Yelp) |
| Paid | Users pay, revenue buys ads, new users |
| Sales | Revenue, hire reps, more customers (HubSpot, Zendesk) |

### Step 3.3 — Three Strategic Levers

1. **Optimize existing loops:** Build micro model, plug hypothetical maxes, find constraints, unconstrain (e.g., speed up cycles, raise capital)
2. **Add new loops:** Order of operations matters; one loop may enable another. Requires new features/products (product-channel fit).
3. **Linear channels:** Use sparingly — for activation energy, feeding loops, or high-intent/low-volume (e.g., head-term SEO)

**Warning:** Adding loops is hard. Loops combine product + channel + model. You can't "slap on" a channel without product changes.

## 5. Phase 4: Monetization Strategy {#5-phase-4-monetization}

### Step 4.1 — Monetization Is More Than Price

Four components (each adds or reduces friction):

- **How** you charge (ads vs subscription vs transaction)
- **When** you charge (upfront vs freemium)
- **What** you charge for (known patterns vs new)
- **Amount** you charge

### Step 4.2 — Four Fits Framework

To build a $100M+ product, align:

- **Product-market fit:** Problem, persona, value prop
- **Product-channel fit:** Product built for channel (UGC for SEO, quick value for virality)
- **Model-channel fit:** Low friction model with low CAC channels; high friction with high CAC
- **Model-product fit:** Product enables model; model enables habit establishment
- **Model-market fit:** Market size times willingness to pay is at least model requirements

### Step 4.3 — Pricing Analysis

- Value metric (what you charge for)
- Price structure (how it scales)
- Price amount (number)

Balance: Perceived Value vs Perceived Price + Friction. Improve value, reduce price perception, reduce friction.

## 6. Phase 5: Build Your Growth Model {#6-phase-5-growth-model}

A growth model is a qualitative or quantitative representation of how your product grows. Answer: **"How does your product grow?"**

### Step 5.1 — Qualitative Growth Model

1. Define **output** (primary metric: e.g., MAU, MRR)
2. Add **habit loops** (output loops that drive retention)
3. Add **acquisition loops** and **linear channels**
4. Show double loops, split models, variations

Goal: Easy to communicate; align team; identify strategic picture.

### Step 5.2 — Loop-Specific Quant Model

For one loop: define loop steps, outputs, conversion formula, variables. Use to isolate impact of a single loop.

### Step 5.3 — End-to-End Quant Model

Bring all loops together: key outputs/inputs, baselines, projections. Use for predictions, goal-setting, prioritization, communication.

### Step 5.4 — Put the Model Into Practice

- **Predictions:** Peer into possible futures
- **Goal-setting:** Paths to reach goals
- **Prioritization:** Compare X vs Y initiatives
- **Communication:** Explain why something matters
- **Update:** Model is a working hypothesis; evolve as you learn

## 7. Phase 6: Apply User Psychology (ELMR + Psych) {#7-phase-6-user-psychology}

Growth model = **what**. User psychology = **why**. You need both.

### Step 6.1 — The Decision Hill (ELMR)

Every action is a climb. Users need:

- **Emotion:** Fuel that drives the action (leads; logic justifies)
- **Logic:** Rational justification (features, stats, reliability, price)
- **Motivation:** Ability + motivation to complete the action
- **Reward:** Confirms right decision; increases future actions (extrinsic, intrinsic, social)

### Step 6.2 — Psych Framework

Measure user motivation as a "fuel tank":

- **Negative psych:** Consumes fuel (friction, cognitive load, confusion)
- **Positive psych:** Adds fuel (desire, clarity, trust)

For each step in a key flow: list elements, assign plus/minus psych, identify net. Find steps that drain fuel or lack positive psych.

### Step 6.3 — Improve the Flow

- Add positive psych where motivation is low
- Reduce negative psych where fuel is drained
- Use urgency, scarcity, bargain, belonging for motivational boosts

## 8. Phase 7: Run Experiments {#8-phase-7-experiments}

Experiments get you to the truth. They avoid human bias (overestimating success, inflating impact) and cut through noise (seasonality, bugs, acquisition/product/competitive changes).

### Step 7.1 — Define Hypotheses

Four components (Experiment Map):

1. **Problem:** What are you trying to solve?
2. **Hypothesis:** What do you believe to be true? (Not a prediction — a belief.)
3. **Evidence:** What backs this up?
4. **Predictions:** If true, what will happen?

### Step 7.2 — Design Minimum Viable Tests (MVTs)

- **Scope:** One question per test
- **Design:** Valid and efficient
- **Success metrics:** What indicates hypothesis is true?
- **Trade-off metrics:** What might get worse?

### Step 7.3 — Prioritize MVTs

Score: Effort, Probability, Upside, Sample size/Runtime. Use scorecards:

- Slam Dunks: Low dev, high EV
- Quickies: Low dev, low EV
- Moonshots: High dev, high EV
- Turtles: High dev, low EV

(Or use ICE: Impact, Confidence, Ease.)

### Step 7.4 — Analyze & Learn

- When to call a result
- Analyze results (not just top-line)
- Apply learnings back to growth model and user psychology map

## 9. Phase 8: Build Defensibility {#9-phase-8-defensibility}

Defensibility is a macro loop that accelerates your underlying loops. Four forms:

1. **Network effects:** Value increases with more users (most important)
2. **Brand:** Trust, recognition, switching cost
3. **Embedding:** Deep integration into customer workflows
4. **Scale:** Cost, distribution, or data advantages

Loops alone aren't enough long-term; tactics get copied. Defensibility makes loops harder to replicate.

## 10. Quick Reference: End-to-End Checklist {#10-quick-reference}

- Define use case (problem, persona, frequency, core action)
- Define retention metric and visualize with cohorts
- Map organic habit loop (trigger, action, reward)
- Design manufactured and environment reinforcement
- Map acquisition loops (viral, content, paid, sales)
- Ensure product-channel and model-channel fit
- Apply Four Fits to monetization
- Build qualitative, then loop quant, then end-to-end growth model
- Apply ELMR and Psych to key flows
- Define hypotheses, design MVTs, prioritize, run, learn
- Identify and strengthen defensibility (network effects, brand, embedding, scale)
